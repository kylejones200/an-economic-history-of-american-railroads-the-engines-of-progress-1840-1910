## Business context

When the first steam locomotive thundered across American rails in the 1830s, it heralded more than just a new way to travel --- it marked the birth of modern American capitalism. Between 1840 and 1910, railroads transformed from scattered local lines into the nation's first large-scale private enterprises, pioneering innovations in business organization, finance, and law that would define American commerce for centuries to come. This analysis examines how railroads fundamentally altered the economy of the United States, driving the shift from small employee shops to giant companies and establishing modern systems of accounting and management.

Unlike earlier transportation projects such as canals, railroads were largely privately funded, marking a significant shift in infrastructure development. Prior to 1850, about 25% of capital needed to build and operate the railroads came from the government, with the remaining 75% derived from loans and stock offerings. Many railroad stocks were owned by overseas investors, contributing to the globalization of American finance.

The impact on capital markets was profound. In 1830, only 36 shares were traded on the New York Stock Exchange. By 1850, thanks to the public offering of railroad stocks, the stock market was flourishing. This investment boom allowed U.S. railroad companies to lay more miles of track by 1860 than the rest of the world combined, totaling over 30,000 miles of rail.
